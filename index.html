“It matters what stories make worlds, what worlds make stories.”

Myths are stories; considered and condensed, with details and perspectives omitted by a mythmaker. The ability of myth as a tool is that it can distort and change what an image or phrase signifies; erasing complexities of human actions and creating worlds without contradiction. This power to erase meaning is Roland Barthes’ motive for labelling myth as dangerous.

The example Barthes uses to demonstrate myths ability to hijack and distort the meaning of an image or phrase is presented in Myth Today. The reader is presented a description of a French propaganda poster depicting a black soldier from a French colony giving a French salute. The intention of this propaganda is to claim there is unity across the French colonies. With a semiological deconstruction of this image we can identify that the signifier is a black soldier, the signified is French patriotism and the sign is the myth that there is unity across the French colonies. Our unease with myth lies in the fact that in reading the posters mythic metalanguage this image as a consequence of human actions and the related histories of trauma and colonisation are erased.

Roland Boer writes in The Robbery of Language that the crafting of “a world without contradiction” is an undeniable acknowledgement of the undercurrent of oppositional perspectives erased in favour of a singular vision. Boer posits that within myth there are opportunities for using counter-myth as a means to resist the erasure of other perspectives.

Myth & Signification invites four artists to approach myth and counter-myth as building blocks for crafting oppositional worlds to contest dominant discourses and resist the homogenisation of perspective. resist the exploitation of land and labour and resist the gendering of bodies. Astrid Elouise Bell is pulled towards the subjects for her triptych of paintings, and her considered practice of rummaging and reflection studies subjects with their own myths. Bell tbh employs painting as a tool for expanding the myth of each subject in a new direction, multiplying their mythology with each brushstroke. Sydney Jarrett’s work also engages with similar ideas of furthering mythologies. Jarrett’s installation references Jordy Rosenberg’s Confession of the Fox and explores how one can trace the creation of myth through archival histories while also extending upon stories that can serve as vehicles for truth. Harrison Rae’s textiles and found objects continues his research into the myth of John Busby. Rae’s work contests the quiet reverence placed upon Busby and the erasure of what was forced upon prisoners and labourers working on Busby’s Bore - a waterway under Oxford Street connecting Centennial Park to Hyde Park. Finally the video work by Nic Narapiromkwan Foo documents the artist praying at the site of Kits on River Road. The documentation is a means to consolidate the demolished restaurant as its own myth and serves as a reminder of myths’ relationship with ritual and memory.

Together these works craft their own world of contradiction through counter myth. A world resisting the homogenisation of perspectives, the gendering of bodies, and the exploitation of land and labour.

Harrison Rae

Harrison Rae is an artist and musician working on Gadigal land. His practice reflects upon the poetics of ordinary objects and histories, approaching storytelling through mixed media and installation.

Sydney Jarrett

Sydney Jarrett is a sculptor working on Gadigal land. His practice explores the semiotics & structures of regulatory bodies and systems of control. He is materially engaged in practices of loitering and gestures of refusal.

Nic Narapiromkwan Foo

Nic Narapiromkwan Foo is an artist and writer eating and conversing on Gadigal land. Having grown up in her parents’ suburban-Australian Thai restaurant, she thinks about boundaries and thresholds and how cooking and eating may be used to negotiate them. As someone who speaks Thai but cannot read or write it, her practice centres oral histories, learning through ritual, and collaborating with family.

Astrid Elouise Bell

Astrid Elouise Bell is a painter, writer and general rummager who lives and works between the lands of the Dharawal and Gadigal people. Her practice surrounds things that elude our grasp and the sites and stories that bring us to our work and each other. Always attempting to find ritual in routine, collective in the familial and histories within the object. She likes collecting, making, and talking; looking for the phenomena of the everyday that is obscured by habit.
