“It matters what stories make worlds, what worlds make stories.”

Myths are stories. However, they are stories, considered and condensed, with finer details and other perspectives omitted by a mythmaker. The erasure of complexity for the benefit of storytelling is the motive behind Roland Barthes calling myth dangerous. In Barthes’ analysis of myth, he begins with a demarcation of form from content. Following this, he states that myth is a form of speech, and therefore as a form of speech, it belongs to the study of linguistic semiology. Following his identification of myth and the necessary mode of analysis, Barthes draws out the relationship between Myth & Signification demonstrating this through the diagram below.

To briefly elucidate the above diagram: there are two semiological systems, language and a metalanguage that from hereon will be called myth. The system called language is what we use to communicate at a base level. Contained within the sign is meaning and without understanding the signifier or the signified we cannot communicate anything to another. At its simplest level, a friend's frowning face is a signifier, what is signified is the friend's negative emotion and the sign that we read from our friend's expression is that they are feeling a negative emotion. Building upon this relationship between signifier, signified and sign we are able to communicate with one another.

As a metalanguage myth uses ordinary language as a tool to build its own system. Myth is effective at emptying the meaning of the sign as it becomes a signifier in the metalanguage system. Therefore the metalanguage can sign something that contradicts the original meaning of the sign. The example Barthes uses in his text is a black soldier from a French colony giving a French salute. The intention of this propaganda is the propagation of the myth of unity across the French colonies. Barthes' unease with myth lies in the fact that the histories of trauma and colonisation are erased.

Barthes' concerns are that mythmakers use myth as a tool to abolish the complexity of human actions, and therefore the consequences that we continue to meet with are explained away as unavoidable. The intention of the mythmakers according to Barthes is the creation of a world without contradiction. Despite the idyllic tone of a world without contradiction the effect is the dismissal of other perspectives. Roland Boer echoes this in The Robbery of Language writing that the crafting of “a world without contradiction” is an undeniable acknowledgement of the undercurrent of oppositional perspectives being erased in favour of a singular vision. However Boer posits that within myth there are opportunities for using counter-myth as a means to resist the world becoming singular.

Myth & Signification invites four artists to approach myth and counter-myth as building blocks for crafting oppositional worlds that contest dominant discourses. Astrid Elouise Bell is pulled towards the subjects for her triptych of paintings. Her considered practice of rummaging and reflection studies subjects with their own myths and employs painting as a tool for extending the myth of each subject in a new direction, multiplying their mythology with each brushstroke. Sydney Jarrett’s work also engages with similar ideas of furthering mythologies. Jarrett’s installation references Jordy Rosenberg’s Confession of the Fox and explores how one can trace the creation of myth through archival histories while also extending upon stories that can serve as vehicles for truth. Harrison Rae’s textiles and found objects continues his research into the myth of John Busby. Rae’s work contests the quiet reverence placed upon Busby and the erasure of what was forced upon prisoners and labourers working on Busby’s Bore - a waterway under Oxford Street connecting Centennial Park to Hyde Park. Finally the video work by Nic Narapiromkwan Foo documents the artist praying at the site of Kits on River Road. The documentation is a means to consolidate the demolished restaurant as its own myth and serves as a reminder of myths’ relationship with ritual and memory.

Harrison Rae

Harrison Rae is an artist and musician working on Gadigal land. His practice reflects upon the poetics of ordinary objects and histories, approaching storytelling through mixed media and installation.

Sydney Jarrett

Sydney Jarrett is a sculptor working on Gadigal land. His practice explores the semiotics & structures of regulatory bodies and systems of control. He is materially engaged in practices of loitering and gestures of refusal.

Nic Narapiromkwan Foo

Nic Narapiromkwan Foo is an artist and writer eating and conversing on Gadigal land. Having grown up in her parents’ suburban-Australian Thai restaurant, she thinks about boundaries and thresholds and how cooking and eating may be used to negotiate them. As someone who speaks Thai but cannot read or write it, her practice centres oral histories, learning through ritual, and collaborating with family.

Astrid Elouise Bell

Astrid Elouise Bell is a painter, writer and general rummager who lives and works between the lands of the Dharawal and Gadigal people. Her practice surrounds things that elude our grasp and the sites and stories that bring us to our work and each other. Always attempting to find ritual in routine, collective in the familial and histories within the object. She likes collecting, making, and talking; looking for the phenomena of the everyday that is obscured by habit.
